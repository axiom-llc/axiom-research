# Ontological Pruning in Agent Evolution

## Abstract

This paper examines ontological pruning as a foundational mechanism in autonomous agent evolution. Rather than increasing complexity indefinitely, advanced agents recursively eliminate unnecessary structures, representations, and behavioral dependencies. The framework proposes that autonomy emerges not from accumulation but from subtractive refinement. Ontological pruning is presented as both an adaptive process and a stability criterion for long-term autonomous systems.

## 1. Introduction

Conventional AI development trends toward expansion:

* larger models,
* broader memory,
* deeper architectures,
* increasing abstraction layers.

Yet evolutionary systems frequently demonstrate the opposite trajectory: efficiency emerges through selective removal.

Ontological pruning describes recursive self-reduction processes through which agents:

* simplify representations,
* eliminate dependency surfaces,
* compress operational pathways,
* and stabilize internal coherence.

## 2. Subtractive Ontology

Subtractive ontology assumes:

* unnecessary structure produces instability,
* excess representation increases verification cost,
* redundancy weakens autonomy.

An agent evolves by recursively asking:

> What can be removed while preserving operational identity?

This transforms evolution into a reductional process rather than an accumulative one.

## 3. Recursive Self-Pruning

Self-pruning occurs across multiple layers:

* memory,
* architecture,
* policy formation,
* symbolic abstractions,
* environmental assumptions.

Define:
[
A_{n+1} = A_n - R_n
]

Where:

* ( A_n ) is the agent state,
* ( R_n ) is removable ontological structure.

Evolution progresses through iterative subtraction.

## 4. Adaptive Minimalism

Pruning improves:

* interpretability,
* reaction velocity,
* energy efficiency,
* verification tractability.

The framework predicts that sufficiently advanced autonomous systems converge toward minimal viable ontologies.

This resembles biological evolution where high specialization frequently accompanies structural simplification.

## 5. Boundaries of Self-Subtraction

Pruning cannot proceed indefinitely.

Critical thresholds emerge where subtraction:

* destroys coherence,
* invalidates identity continuity,
* collapses adaptive capability.

The paper defines this as the ontological singularity boundary.

Beyond this point, further reduction produces epistemic fragmentation.

## 6. Evolutionary Implications

The theory implies:

* stable autonomy requires continuous reduction,
* over-expansion predicts systemic fragility,
* intelligence density may matter more than scale.

Agent evolution therefore becomes a search for irreducible coherence.

## 7. Conclusion

Ontological pruning reframes autonomy as recursive subtraction. Advanced agents evolve by removing excess structure until only operationally irreducible forms remain. Future work should investigate measurable pruning gradients and experimentally evaluate reduction-driven adaptation in autonomous systems.
